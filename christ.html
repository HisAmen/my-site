✝️ They Called Him God

Direct statements · No ambiguity
“Thomas answered and said to Him, 'My Lord and my God!'” — John 20:28

Thomas fell at Jesus' feet after the resurrection and called Him "my God." Jesus accepted it. No correction. No "don't worship me." A faithful Jew would never let that pass unless it was true. He didn't flinch.

“But unto the Son He saith, 'Thy throne, O God, is for ever and ever.'” — Hebrews 1:8

The Father Himself addresses the Son and calls Him God. Not a parable. Not a metaphor. The Father says it. That's about as clear as it gets.

“In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God... And the Word was made flesh.” — John 1:1,14

John opens his Gospel with this. He had walked with Jesus for three years. He ate with Him. He saw Him die. He saw Him risen. He knew exactly what he was saying. The Word was God. Then the Word became a man.

“This is the true God, and eternal life.” — 1 John 5:20

At the end of his first letter, John writes this. The entire sentence points to Jesus Christ. No ambiguity. No wiggle room.

“Christ came, who is over all, God blessed for ever.” — Romans 9:5

Paul, writing to his fellow Jews in Rome, describes Christ as "God blessed for ever." He wasn't writing poetry. He was making a theological statement about who Jesus is. A monotheistic Jew doesn't write that unless he means it.

“Looking for that blessed hope, and the glorious appearing of the great God and our Saviour Jesus Christ.” — Titus 2:13

The Greek grammar here makes it impossible to read this as two separate beings. One person is in view. "The great God and our Saviour" — same person. Paul says Jesus is the great God.

“Through the righteousness of God and our Saviour Jesus Christ.” — 2 Peter 1:1

Peter opens his second letter the same way. Peter — the man who denied Jesus three times, the man who walked with Him daily, the man who was crucified upside down for his faith — calls Jesus God.

“Thou shalt call His name Emmanuel, which being interpreted is, God with us.” — Matthew 1:23

Before Jesus was even born, the angel told Joseph the child would be called Emmanuel — "God with us." His name was a claim to deity from the very beginning. They named Him God with us.

🔥 Jesus Claimed the Name of God

I AM · The divine name
“And God said unto Moses, 'I AM THAT I AM.'” — Exodus 3:14

At the burning bush, Moses asked God His name. God answered, "I AM THAT I AM." This is the sacred name, YHWH. It was so holy that Jews wouldn't even speak it aloud. They substituted "Adonai" (Lord) because the name was too sacred.

“Jesus said unto them, 'Verily, verily, I say unto you, Before Abraham was, I AM.'” — John 8:58-59

Centuries later, Jesus stands in the temple arguing with religious leaders. They ask if He thinks He's greater than Abraham. Jesus answers, "Before Abraham was, I AM." He didn't say "I was." He used the present tense of the divine name. The crowd immediately grabbed stones to kill Him for blasphemy. Nobody misunderstood what He was claiming.

They didn't pick up stones because He said He was old. They picked up stones because He said He was God.

⚡ Jesus Did Things Only God Can Do

Forgive sins · Judge nations · Raise Himself
“Jesus said unto the sick of the palsy, 'Son, thy sins be forgiven thee.'” — Mark 2:5-7

A paralyzed man was lowered through a roof. Jesus looked at him and said, "Your sins are forgiven." The religious leaders whispered, "Who can forgive sins but God alone?" They were right. That was the point. Jesus was making a claim. Then He healed the man to prove He had the authority.

“When the Son of Man shall come in His glory... before Him shall be gathered all nations.” — Matthew 25:31-32

The Old Testament says God alone will gather the nations and judge them. Joel 3:2 says the Lord will sit in judgment. Jesus says the Son of Man will sit on a throne, gather all nations, and separate them like sheep from goats. Same scene. Same role. Same person.

“Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up... I have power to lay my life down, and I have power to take it again.” — John 2:19, John 10:18

Jesus said He would raise Himself from the dead. Nobody talks like this unless they believe they hold authority over life and death. Dead people don't raise themselves. He claimed the power to do it.

“I am the good shepherd.” — John 10:11,14

God promised in Ezekiel that He Himself would be the shepherd of His people. Psalm 23 says the LORD is the shepherd. Then Jesus announces, "I am the good shepherd." He doesn't say He's a helper or an assistant. He takes the title God reserved for Himself.

📜 Old Testament Prophecies — The Messiah Would Be God

Isaiah · Micah · Zechariah
“Unto us a child is born... and His name shall be called Wonderful, Counsellor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father.” — Isaiah 9:6

Isaiah says a child will be born and His name will be called "Mighty God, Everlasting Father." The Messiah was never meant to be just a man. The prophecy explicitly calls Him God.

“Whose goings forth have been from of old, from everlasting.” — Micah 5:2

Micah says the coming ruler's "goings forth have been from of old, from everlasting." The Messiah existed before time. That's not language you use for a created being. That's eternal existence.

“They shall look upon Me whom they have pierced.” — Zechariah 12:10

Zechariah records God Himself speaking: "They shall look upon Me whom they have pierced." YHWH says they will pierce Him. When Jesus was crucified, John writes that this verse was fulfilled. They pierced Jesus. They pierced God. (John 19:37)

“He was wounded for our transgressions... He bare the sin of many.” — Isaiah 53:5,12

Isaiah 53 describes a Servant who bears sin, is pierced, buried with the rich, and intercedes for sinners. Only God can forgive sins, and this Servant does exactly that. Jesus fulfills every detail. The early church didn't invent this. It's right there in the Hebrew Scriptures.

📖 "Firstborn" — About Rank, Not Origin

Colossians 1:15 explained
“Who is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of every creature.” — Colossians 1:15

Critics point to this verse and say it means Jesus was created. The word "firstborn" doesn't work that way in Scripture. Let me show you.

“Also I will make him my firstborn, higher than the kings of the earth.” — Psalm 89:27

David was the youngest son of Jesse. He had older brothers. Yet God calls him "firstborn." Everyone reading knows it means highest rank, not birth order.

“I have caused Ephraim to come forth as my firstborn.” — Jeremiah 31:9

Ephraim was the younger brother. Manasseh was born first. Yet God calls Ephraim "firstborn." Same pattern. Rank, not chronology.

“Israel is my son, my firstborn.” — Exodus 4:22

Israel was not the first nation. Egypt, Babylon, and others existed before. But God calls Israel His firstborn. The word means chosen, exalted, preeminent. Always has.

Paul knew this. When he called Jesus the firstborn of creation, he meant Jesus outranks it. He is preeminent over everything. The very next verse says Jesus created everything. You can't create everything and be part of it.

“For by Him were all things created, that are in heaven, and that are in earth.” — Colossians 1:16

🕊️ One Last Thing — He Raised Himself

The ultimate claim
“Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up.” — John 2:19
“If thou shalt confess with thy mouth the Lord Jesus, and shalt believe in thine heart that God raised Him from the dead.” — Romans 10:9

Jesus said He would raise Himself from the dead. Paul says the Father raised Him. Both are true. Same event. Same power. Same God. The Trinity isn't a contradiction. It's the only way to make sense of what the text actually says.
